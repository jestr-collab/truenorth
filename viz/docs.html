Spatial Fingerprint

Understanding the Spatial Fingerprint

The Spatial Fingerprint is built to show where a mix makes its strongest spatial statements. Rather than plotting every sound that exists in a track, it highlights the moments where the mix clearly asserts itself - the points where energy, placement, and clarity combine to create a noticeable spatial impression.

Each dot represents a perceptually important moment. These are typically transient-anchored events such as vocal syllables, drum hits, guitar attacks, piano notes, or any element that naturally pulls listener focus. By concentrating on these moments, the fingerprint provides a meaningful snapshot of the mix's spatial decisions over time.

Why some sounds do not appear on the graph

Music often contains continuous textures: reverb tails, ambient pads, stereo wash, room tone, chorus effects, and other sustained layers. These sounds shape the emotional atmosphere of a recording, but because they evolve gradually and lack clear transient definition, they do not always register as dominant analysis events.

If every continuous sound were plotted, the visualization would quickly become a dense cloud of points with no clear structure. The Spatial Fingerprint intentionally avoids this by focusing on perceptual salience rather than constant signal activity.

A useful mindset: a dot does not mean there is sound here - it means the mix made a spatial decision here.

Understanding glow (spatial bloom)

Some points appear with a soft glow. This indicates that sound energy continues to expand after the initial impact. In practical terms, glowing points often correspond to reverb tails, room decay, stereo delay reflections, or sustained harmonic bloom.

Glow does not represent loudness or width by itself. Instead, it reflects how long sound energy persists after the transient. Dry, tight sounds typically produce solid dots, while more atmospheric or spacious moments produce glowing ones.

Using Region Stats

The Region Stats panel allows you to click and drag across the visualization to study a focused area of the mix. This provides a deeper layer of insight by summarizing the spatial behavior of selected moments.

  • Artifact Count: Number of perceptually dominant events in the selected region.
  • Relative Density: Percent of total events in the selected region.
  • Frequency Mix: Low/mid/high dominance balance among selected events.
  • Mean Presence: Average perceptual intensity of events in that region.
  • Stereo Bias: Average left-right positioning tendency of selected events.
  • Mean Wetness: Average spatial decay character (ambience and bloom).

Interpreting sparse vs dense fingerprints

A sparse fingerprint does not mean a mix lacks detail. It often indicates arrangements that rely on atmosphere, sustain, or gradual dynamic build. Dense clusters often reflect productions with strong transient definition and deliberate placement choices.

Getting the most value

Use the fingerprint alongside loudness, crest factor, and low-end analysis to build a complete picture of how your mix behaves. The goal is not to replace listening, but to support it. Over time, you will begin to recognize visual patterns that correspond to mixes that feel punchy, wide, intimate, or atmospheric.

Loudness Analysis

Loudness analysis measures the perceived loudness of your audio over time using LUFS (Loudness Units relative to Full Scale). This helps ensure your mix meets broadcast standards and maintains consistent perceived volume.

Understanding LUFS

LUFS is an industry-standard measurement that accounts for human perception of loudness, unlike simple peak or RMS measurements. It provides a more accurate representation of how loud your audio actually sounds to listeners.

Key Features

  • Integrated LUFS: Overall loudness measurement for the entire track
  • Short-term LUFS: Dynamic loudness changes throughout the track
  • Track vs Reference comparison: Match your track's loudness to reference material
  • Time-based visualization: See how loudness evolves over time
  • Hover tooltips: Get precise LUFS values at any point in time

How to Use

After uploading your audio, the Loudness curve will display the short-term LUFS values over time. Compare with your reference track to identify sections that need adjustment. Aim for consistent loudness levels throughout your mix.

Crest Factor

Crest factor measures the ratio between peak and RMS levels, indicating the dynamic range and transient content of your audio. Higher crest factors suggest more punch and less compression, while lower values indicate a more compressed, squashed sound.

Understanding Crest Factor

Crest factor is calculated as the difference between peak and RMS levels in decibels. A high crest factor means your audio has strong transients and dynamic range, while a low crest factor indicates heavy compression or limiting has been applied.

Key Features

  • Real-time crest visualization: See crest factor changes throughout your track
  • Punch measurement: Additional metric showing transient contrast
  • Track vs Reference comparison: Identify if your mix is over-compressed
  • Time-based analysis: Pinpoint sections with different dynamic characteristics
  • Interactive tooltips: View exact crest and punch values at any time point

How to Use

Use the Crest visualization to ensure your mix maintains appropriate dynamic range. Compare against your reference to see if you've over-compressed. Sections with very low crest factors may sound lifeless and lack punch.

Low End Analysis

Low End analysis tracks the low-frequency energy in your mix over time. This helps identify boomy sections, weak bass areas, and ensures proper low-end balance throughout your track.

Understanding Low End Energy

Low end energy is measured in the sub-bass and bass frequency ranges. This visualization shows how low-frequency content varies throughout your track, helping you maintain consistent bass presence and avoid muddiness or weak sections.

Key Features

  • Normalized low-end energy: Values range from 0 to 1 for easy comparison
  • Time-based visualization: See how bass energy evolves throughout the track
  • Track vs Reference comparison: Match low-end levels to your reference
  • Hover tooltips: Get precise low-end values at any point in time
  • Smooth curve visualization: Easy to spot trends and problem areas

How to Use

After analysis, review the Low End curve to identify sections with excessive or insufficient bass. Compare with your reference track to ensure consistent low-end balance. Look for sudden spikes that might indicate boomy frequencies or drops that suggest weak bass.

Getting Started

New to Mix Affinity? Follow these steps to get the most out of your audio analysis.

Quick Start Guide

  • Upload your main track: Select a WAV file to analyze
  • Add a reference (optional): Compare against a professional mix
  • Name your session: Give your analysis a descriptive name
  • Click Analyze: Wait for processing to complete
  • Explore visualizations: Switch between different analysis views
  • Compare Track vs Reference: Toggle between your mix and reference
  • Select regions: Click and drag on Spatial Fingerprint to see statistics

Best Practices

For best results, use high-quality WAV files at 44.1kHz or higher. Reference tracks should be professionally mixed and mastered material in a similar genre. Take time to understand each visualization and how it relates to your mix decisions.
