
Understanding the Spatial Fingerprint

The Spatial Fingerprint is built to show where a mix makes its strongest spatial statements. 
Rather than plotting every sound that exists in a track, it highlights the moments where the mix 
clearly asserts itself — the points where energy, placement, and clarity combine to create a 
noticeable spatial impression.

Each dot represents a perceptually important moment. These are typically transient‑anchored events 
such as vocal syllables, drum hits, guitar attacks, piano notes, or any element that naturally 
pulls listener focus. By concentrating on these moments, the fingerprint provides a meaningful 
snapshot of the mix’s spatial decisions over time.

Why you may hear sounds that do not appear on the graph

Music often contains continuous textures: reverb tails, ambient pads, stereo wash, room tone, 
chorus effects, and other sustained layers. These sounds shape the emotional atmosphere of a 
recording, but because they evolve gradually and lack clear transient definition, they do not 
always register as dominant analysis events.

If every continuous sound were plotted, the visualization would quickly become a dense cloud 
of points with no clear structure. The Spatial Fingerprint intentionally avoids this by focusing 
on perceptual salience rather than constant signal activity.

A helpful way to think about it:

A dot does not mean “there is sound here.”  
A dot means “the mix made a spatial decision here.”

Understanding glow (spatial bloom)

Some points appear with a soft glow. This indicates that sound energy continues to expand after 
the initial impact. In practical terms, glowing points often correspond to reverb tails, room 
decay, stereo delay reflections, or sustained harmonic bloom.

Glow does not represent loudness or width by itself. Instead, it reflects how long sound energy 
persists after the transient. Dry, tight sounds typically produce solid dots, while more 
atmospheric or spacious moments produce glowing ones.

Using Region Stats to analyze specific sections

The Region Stats panel allows you to click and drag across the visualization to study a focused 
area of the mix. This provides a deeper layer of insight by summarizing the spatial behavior of 
selected moments.

Artifact Count  
The number of perceptually dominant events inside the selected region. Higher counts suggest 
greater rhythmic clarity or spatial activity within that area.

Relative Density  
The percentage of total events that fall inside the selection. This helps you understand how 
important that region is compared to the overall mix timeline.

Frequency Mix  
The balance of low, mid, and high frequency dominance among the selected events. A low‑heavy 
region may indicate bass‑driven spatial focus, while mid‑heavy regions often relate to vocals 
or guitars.

Mean Presence  
The average perceptual intensity of events in the region. Higher values generally indicate 
moments where elements feel more forward or assertive in the mix.

Stereo Bias  
The average left‑to‑right positioning tendency within the selected area. Values near zero 
suggest centered balance, while more extreme values reveal directional weighting.

Mean Wetness  
The average spatial decay characteristic of the region. Higher wetness typically reflects 
more ambience, longer reverbs, or sustained spatial bloom.

Interpreting sparse versus dense fingerprints

A sparse fingerprint does not mean a mix lacks detail. It often indicates arrangements that rely 
on atmosphere, sustain, or gradual dynamic build. Dense clusters often reflect productions with 
strong transient definition and deliberate placement choices.

Getting the most value from the Spatial Fingerprint

Use the fingerprint alongside loudness, crest factor, and low‑end analysis to build a complete 
picture of how your mix behaves. The goal is not to replace listening, but to support it. Over 
time, you will begin to recognize visual patterns that correspond to mixes that feel punchy, 
wide, intimate, or atmospheric.
